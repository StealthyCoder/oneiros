# First real run — findings

**2026-07-23.** First multi-cycle run of the Phase 1 feedback loop against
real infrastructure: `python scripts/dream.py --max-spend 1`, 164 cycles,
default `--blend-weight 0.5`, run identifier `20260723_092712`. Total spend
$1.00ish, average narration length 218.6 words. Run output (`output/dreams/`)
is gitignored and not preserved in the repo - this note records the
aggregate findings, not the underlying images/narratives themselves.

**Framing, up front.** This is a first look, not evidence of anything
designed or intended. The root README's Phase 4 section is explicit that a
noise-injected feedback loop will produce recurring motifs over a
long-enough run for purely statistical reasons - regression to modes
already present in the underlying models' training data, not a signal of
anything emergent - and that "a pattern nobody designed" only means
something measured against a control run without the narrative feeding
back in, which this project hasn't built yet. Several findings below are
exactly that kind of drift, caught and quantified, not counter-examples to
the caution.

## Narrative text: a strong, stable "voice" — not memory, not drift

Every one of the 164 narrations is a fully independent, stateless API
call - Claude has zero memory of any prior narration in the run, only the
current cycle's image. So any similarity across narrations can only come
from Claude's own output distribution under the fixed system prompt and
temperature=1.0, not from anything carried over cycle to cycle.

That distribution turns out to be remarkably consistent. "like" appears in
**164/164** narrations, em-dashes in **164/164**, "everything" in 90.9%,
"something" in 80.5%, "there's" in 67.1%. **87.2%** of narrations open with
just "The" (45.7%) or "I'm" (41.5%). The single clearest illustration: two
entirely unrelated, non-adjacent cycles converged on nearly the same
sentence from nothing -

> Cycle 22: *"The air tastes like concrete and waiting."*
> Cycle 42: *"The air tastes like concrete dust and waiting."*

- more broadly, some variant of "\[the\] air tastes like X" appears in
14.6% of all narrations. Recurring vocabulary clusters around
light/white/breathing (roughly half the corpus each) and skeletal/
anatomical imagery - bones, ribs, spine, veins, throat (~40%+ combined).

Despite that strength, there's **no decay or drift**: vocabulary diversity
(type-token ratio) is flat between the first 30 and last 30 cycles (0.4085
vs 0.4115), no two narrations are phrase-level near-duplicates across all
13,366 possible pairs (max bigram-set overlap: 0.0227), and nothing trends
directionally over the run - word count, rhetorical-question rate, and
motif frequency all stay within noise. It's a fingerprint that gets
reproduced fresh every cycle, not a trajectory the loop is on.

## Images: no perceptual collapse, mild population-level bias

Across all 13,366 possible image pairs, the **maximum** pairwise
perceptual similarity was 0.7656 (cycles 78 and 159) against a 0.90
near-duplicate threshold - **zero pairs** cleared it. Mean pairwise
similarity across the whole set: 0.501 ± 0.061. The imagery stays
genuinely diverse for all 164 cycles.

There is a real population-level color bias: the red channel is dominant
in 75% of images (123/164), and the corpus-wide channel means are ordered
R (120.2) > G (113.2) > B (104.1) - a warm-skewed population, not a
uniform one. There's also a slow, gradual drift across the run: brightness
rises slightly (111.19 → 115.13, first 30 vs last 30 cycles), saturation
falls more noticeably (0.286 → 0.210), and the color balance shifts
warm-early toward blue/green-heavier late (blue channel gains the most,
+8.15). No sharp jumps - all linear and gradual. No image was a true
near-black or near-white outlier.

This is the fact that makes the narrative-side convergence *not* worrying:
if the narrative-derived seed were leaking real structure into the noise,
you'd expect image convergence to track narrative convergence. It doesn't
- the text repeats a voice, the images never do. That's the expected
result given `_blend_latents()`'s narrative-derived component is a
SHA256-seeded but still fully independent Gaussian draw (see
[`../todos/active/narrative-latent-influence.md`](../todos/active/narrative-latent-influence.md))
- consistent with the math, now confirmed empirically over a real run
rather than just derived on paper.

## Grounding: narration tracks its own image, with one specific failure mode

A 12-cycle spot-check (spread evenly across the run) comparing each image
against its own narration came back **9 TIGHT / 3 LOOSE / 0 GENERIC** -
narrations are genuinely grounded in the specific image they're describing,
not swappable template prose. A shuffle test (could a shuffled narration be
confidently re-matched to its image using only the text?) succeeded on
roughly 10 of 12.

The 3 LOOSE cases weren't vagueness - they were accurate on most details
plus one confidently-stated, specifically wrong detail. Cycle 105's
narration insists on "tarnished silver... bronze and pewter" for a vessel
that's actually brown glass or wood, while getting the strawberries, green
herbs, and yellow flecks in the same image right. Worth remembering as the
narration stage's failure mode: when it's wrong, it's wrong with
confidence and specificity, not with hedging.

## Content categories: nature-dominant, evenly mixed across the run

All 164 images were classified by visual content only (not narration text,
since narration is known from the above to occasionally invent confident,
wrong specifics) against four non-exclusive categories - person, car,
industry, nature:

| category | count | % of 164 |
|---|---|---|
| nature | 92 | 56.1% |
| person | 46 | 28.0% |
| industry | 34 | 20.7% |
| car | 16 | 9.8% |
| *(none of the four)* | 30 | 18.3% |

**Stable across the run**, same pattern as everything else above - first
half (cycles 1-82) vs second half (83-164) percentages move by at most
2.5 points on any category (nature 54.9%→57.3%, person 26.8%→29.3%,
industry 19.5%→22.0%, car 9.8%→9.8% exactly flat).

**No thematic clustering** - the longest run of consecutive cycles sharing
a category was nature at 6 cycles (starting 0039); person, industry, and
car never exceeded 3 consecutive cycles. The longest stretch with *none*
of the four categories present was only 2 cycles. Categories are well
interleaved throughout, not bunched into runs.

**Co-occurrence**: person+nature is the most common pairing (20 images),
followed by industry+nature (14) and car+nature (11) - unsurprising given
nature's overall dominance, but notably person+industry (8), person+car
(4), and car+industry (4) are all real too, so the four categories aren't
segregated into disjoint scene-types.

**One notable absence-then-presence**: no car appears at all until cycle
21 - the single longest gap for any category (20 consecutive cycles).
Whether that's meaningful or just where a 9.8%-base-rate category happened
to first land in a 164-sample sequence isn't answerable from one run.

## What this settles

- **The 25-inference-steps question** flagged in
  [`../todos/completed/image-generation.md`](../todos/completed/image-generation.md)
  as "worth revisiting once the feedback loop is running." Answered: images
  carry enough real structure for narration to extract specific, checkable
  visual detail 75%+ of the time (9/12 TIGHT). No evidence 25 steps is a
  bottleneck.
- **The noise-floor safety concern** in
  [`../todos/active/blend-weight-gradient.md`](../todos/active/blend-weight-gradient.md).
  Confirmed empirically, not just mathematically: zero perceptual collapse
  across 164 cycles at fixed `blend_weight=0.5`.

## What's still open

The interesting lever this run points at isn't `blend_weight` at all - it's
that Claude's narration style is a strong, stable attractor under this
system prompt and temperature, entirely independent of loop mechanics.
Neither currently-open note
([`narrative-latent-influence.md`](../todos/active/narrative-latent-influence.md),
[`blend-weight-gradient.md`](../todos/active/blend-weight-gradient.md))
tracks that observation - it's a genuinely different question from either
of them and isn't resolved here.
